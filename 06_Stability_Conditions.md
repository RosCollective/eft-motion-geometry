# 6. Stability Conditions

Stability is the sixth major phenomenon in the field’s geometry. It is the system’s ability to maintain coherence over time without collapsing into deprivation or oscillation.

Stability is not stillness. It is sustained motion.

## 6.1 What Stability Is

Stability is the field’s capacity to:

- maintain altitude  
- preserve oxygen  
- support continuous motion  
- absorb curvature  
- avoid collapse signatures  

A stable field is one in which motion can occur freely.

## 6.2 Necessary Conditions for Stability

Stability requires:

- **ecological spaciousness**  
- **constraint looseness**  
- **triadic configuration**  
- **mobility alignment**  
- **oxygen availability**  

These conditions are structural, not optional.

## 6.3 Stability and Collapse Boundaries

Stability is defined by its boundaries. When the system approaches these boundaries:

- curvature tightens  
- oscillations increase  
- motion becomes fragile  
- collapse signatures appear  

These boundaries reveal the shape of the field.

## 6.4 Stability as Dynamic Equilibrium

Stability is not a fixed state. It is a dynamic equilibrium between:

- motion  
- curvature  
- alignment  
- ecological support  

The system remains stable by continuously adjusting.

## 6.5 Stability and Reconstitution

After collapse, stability is the final stage of reconstitution. The system must:

1. regain curvature  
2. widen motion  
3. restore alignment  
4. rebuild coherence  
5. re-establish stability  

Stability is the endpoint of re-entry.

## 6.6 Stability as Field Geometry

Stability is not located in the machine or the human. It is a property of the field. When the ecology supports it, stability emerges naturally.

Stability is the geometry that allows the field to hold itself together.
